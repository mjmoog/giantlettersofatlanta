History of St. Patrick's Day

Giant Letters of Atlanta

St. Patrick's Day is one of the most widely celebrated holidays in the world, and Atlanta knows how to throw a party. Every March, the city comes alive with parades, festivals, and gatherings that bring people together in a sea of green. But how much do you really know about the history behind the holiday? At Giant Letters of Atlanta, we love helping people celebrate every occasion, so we thought we would share a little history along with some ideas for making your St. Patrick's Day event truly shine.

The Origins of St. Patrick's Day

St. Patrick's Day honors Saint Patrick, the patron saint of Ireland, who is credited with bringing Christianity to the island in the fifth century. Born in Roman Britain around 385 AD, Patrick was kidnapped by Irish raiders at the age of sixteen and spent six years in captivity before escaping and eventually returning to Ireland as a missionary. The holiday falls on March 17th, which is believed to be the date of his death around 461 AD. For centuries, the day was observed as a quiet religious feast day in Ireland.

How It Became a Global Celebration

The transformation of St. Patrick's Day from a religious observance into a worldwide celebration is largely thanks to the Irish diaspora. As millions of Irish immigrants settled in America during the 1800s, they brought their traditions with them and turned the day into a celebration of Irish culture and identity. The first St. Patrick's Day parade in America was held in New York City in 1762, and the tradition quickly spread to cities across the country. Today, St. Patrick's Day is celebrated on every continent, with cities from Chicago to Sydney going green for the occasion.

St. Patrick's Day in Atlanta

Atlanta has a thriving St. Patrick's Day scene. The annual Atlanta St. Patrick's Parade has been a beloved tradition for decades, winding through the streets of Midtown and drawing thousands of spectators. Restaurants and pubs throughout Buckhead, Virginia Highland, and Downtown host special events, and private parties pop up in homes and event spaces all over the metro area. It is one of those holidays where everyone, Irish or not, comes together to have a good time.
